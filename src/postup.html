The challenge

With the rise of remote work, PostUpâ€”a startup focused on building an online community for freelancersâ€”observed a growing need for reliable information about public workspaces on their platform. Remote workers often struggle to find suitable locations, such as libraries or coffee shops, wasting time on unsuitable visits due to inadequate online resources. In addition, there is a scarcity of suitable locations.

How can I connect remote workers with suitable public workspaces and drive PostUp's business objectives?

The constraints

I was tasked with designing a subscription-based mobile app for PostUp, priced at $5.99 per month. The available user research was limited, consisting of data from 9 interviews, 8 quotes, a single user persona, and one interview recording.

Resource

  • Limited research materials
  • No access to PostUp personnel
  • No access to current customers

Product and business

  • Mobile-app solution
  • $5.99 monthly subscription fee
  • Feature existing places only

Time

  • Tight 5 day deadline
  • Limited iterations and testing

The Final Solution

In 5 days, I developed an MVP prototype and monetization strategy. It helps remote workers find suitable public spaces by providing detailed information on amenities like WiFi speed and noise levels. My monetization strategy includes a "PostUp Partners" program, offering perks such as real-time seating updates and promotions, enhancing the remote work experience and creating a new revenue stream for PostUp.

Adapting this process for solo work, I was able to efficiently deliver a design and business solution for PostUp.

storyboard
Outlining the critical steps in a remote worker's journey

Drawing from my lightning demos, I conducted a Crazy 8s exercise, sketching 8 frames in 8 minutes to generate solutions. As my first time, this felt intimidating at first, but I was pleasantly surprised by how productive the process was!

Among my Crazy 8's I identified one screen to be the most critical screen and developed it into a 3-panel storyboard. I chose the "search results" screen (the middle screen below) because it is the central hub for remote workers to compare and decide on a workspace.

Since accessing relevant information is a remote worker's primary challenge, my key design consideration was to effectively centralizing essential information to optimize decision-making.

I initially thought the journey ended when users chose a workspace. I later realized it ends when they're settled in (oops).

wednesday
Decide

storyboarding pt.2
Completing the journey with user stories

On day 3, I expanded the 3-panel storyboard into a detailed comic-style narrative of Nina's journey using PostUp in a new city. This helped me immerse myself in Nina's perspective to deepen empathy for her needs.

This was really fun! By leveraging my filmmaking storyboard experience, this felt effective than traditional user flows.

Placing Nina in an unfamiliar city allowed me to anticipate diverse user behaviors and needs.

By asking "How might Nina react?" and "What actions might she take next?", I identified key touchpoints to develop user stories and flows. This approach aligns with my principles of creating scalable and adaptable solutions, which further developed my user flow. Here are some highlights:

What might Nina want to see when she opens PostUp?

Being in a new city, Nina likely has little knowledge of her surroundings, so she's probably keen to explore nearby workspaces. Therefore, the ideal home screen should show Nina a map that visually displays available options around her current location.

Where should Nina "search by task"?

Upon expanding my storyboard, I realized two design errors from days 1 and 2.

Day 1: Optimizing search process by taking users directly to search results after task-selection might backfire if the presets are even slight inaccurate.

Day 2: The lack of transparency behind the presets if located on the 'Discover' page might be confusing.

Solution: Create a 'Search page' that allows people to conduct a 'New search' or select 'Customizable presets'.

What if Nina realizes she has additional needs?

With a new 'Search' page, I was able to display the details on the preset filters to ensure transparency. As for my conflict regarding whether or not Nina should be able to adjust filters immediately after task selection, after reviewing my user research, I realized most people share the same preferences across all tasks! Being taken to a filters page each time Nina selects a preset could be counterproductive. She can also just conduct a new search. Besides, testing may reveal the ideal flow!

In addition to the workspace environment, what else might Nina want to learn?

The final highlight of my storyboard evolution was adding a 'Reviews' section for each workspace. After Nina learns about the workspace's details, she might want to see what other people have to say or seek more details, especially since that's what she's used to doing with her existing tools. For a more intuitive experience, users may navigate between the pages by swiping horizontally.

Single-page for efficient navigation

I consolidated my sketches into a single-page layout to prioritize above-the-fold details, ensuring efficient navigation. To avoid information overload, I used a minimalistic design for readability and stored additional details in "Info" icon buttons. Leveraging social proof, I displayed each reviewer's occupation to enhance trust and engagement, aligning with PostUp's mission to support remote workers.

Comprehensive environment details

To reduce information overload, I crafted minimal, scannable copy, and included several "Info" icon button for additional details. I was inspired by clothing size charts to ensure users have access to essential information without feeling overwhelmed.

Stashed Features

Given limited time and resources, I could only prototype one route. To make the most of my single round of testing, I reviewed my previous case study on my most recent project, TasteBuds. This reminded me that users quickly identify missing essential features but overlook non-essential ones.

Therefore, I focused on developing features with the spanest research evidence and stashed the rest for validation during testing:

  • Seating availability

    This was mentioned by one interviewee. It could expedite a remote worker's journey and monetized.

  • Reservation system

    This has great potential but its rarity in coffee shops is a challenge. It also could be monetized.

  • Home search bar

    As a common user route, I anticipated some users opting for this route instead of the search page.

  • Popular times

    Given its popularity in other apps, it could enhance the UX and boost competitive advantage.

Friday
Test

While this was very helpful as I tackled the screens, sadly, the dilemma I experienced on day 3 did not get fully resolved. I encountered a similar issue while trying to anticipate different user behaviors and potential alternate routes. Realizing I lacked sufficient time to cover them all, I decided against prototyping alternate routes and planned to rely on testing to reveal them.

The usability testing I conducted with my first high-fidelity prototype consisted of 5 users.

Participant Profile

  • College students and working professionals
  • Do remote work in public spaces at least once a week
  • Primary method to find suitable locations is via mobile apps

Objectives

  • Identify unmet needs and unnecessary features
  • Uncover the primary user route(s), behaviors, and expectations
  • Evaluate pending features

Instead of prototyping every possible user flow, I relied on usability testing to validate user interactions. For example, I asked users, "Without touching the screen, how would you proceed to search for a workspace?" This approach allowed me to gather insights on intuitive navigation paths and prioritize features accordingly.

Results

Overall, the feedback I received was overwhelmingly positive. Participants praised the user-friendly navigation, quick access to critical information, and visual design. They didn't find any critical information missing, but some suggested including additional helpful details that they don't find in their current methods, such as lighting and restroom capacity. Everyone completed their task very quickly and successfully.

The only errors occurred were some confusion with the copy and icons, which is reasonable given how much of the information included in this prototype are not typically found in other apps.

Here's an excerpt from the usability test with participant #1, where she offers positive feedback on the prototype after completing her given task:

Business monetization strategy

Based on the usability testing results, I brainstormed potential strategies aligned with the startup's requirement for a $5.99 monthly user subscription fee to increase business revenue streams. My focus involves brainstorming new features that drive spaner engagement with businesses featured on PostUp, thus amplifying revenue generation. Considering the growing trend toward remote work, I see a promising opportunity for a mutually beneficial collaboration between PostUp and local businesses. This collaboration could incentivize businesses to enhance their amenities, catering to remote workers and in turn enriching the overall user experience for Postup subscribers. Therefore, I propose an additional subscription and referral model that invites businesses to become a â€œPostUp Partnerâ€ to access premium in-app business features below:

Exclusive promotions

PostUp partners can offer exclusive in-app promotions, encouraging engagement and customer acquisition. Subscribers may filter for locations with ongoing promotions.

Reservation system

Only PostUp Partners can access the in-app table reservation feature, enhancing user convenience and potentially attracting more customers. Users may filter their search results for locations that offer reservations.

Rewards system

Partners may offer exclusive rewards for users. Using a unique QR code, users may earn points and stamps for their activities, which can be redeemed for tangible rewards, encouraging frequent use and loyalty.

Data analytics

PostUp can offer valuable insights derived from user behavior, like traffic trends and user preferences, enabling partners to better understand their customer base, thereby allowing them to tailor their services and improve customer satisfaction.

Conclusion

Overall, my strategies aim to increase client acquisition, boost conversion, drive app engagement, attract business partnerships, and establish a sustainable revenue model while enhancing the overall remote working experience throughout their user journey. In sum, these strategies offer additional revenue streams for PostUp while boosting the app's value proposition.
