Tools

The challenge

Diners face challenges in confidently discovering dining options and making decisions due to skepticism surrounding crowdsourced reviews. Moreover, the rising commercialization and reduced personalization in popular platforms such as Yelp and Google Maps contribute to information overload and decision paralysis among users.

How might I empower diners to confidently discover new dining options?

The Final Solution

The Final Solution

AI generated personalized recommendations exclusively based on friends and personal preferences

The final solution empowers diners to confidently discover dining options with people they trust. Leveraging word-of-mouth recommendations from friends, Tastebuds provides personalized recommendations based on diners' preferences and dining history.

B2B business model that provides restaurant subscribers access to user data and feedback

To generate a revenue stream without overcommercialization and improve the overall diner journey, TasteBuds invites restaurants to join as business partners to offer exclusive in-app promotions and stay connected with diners.

Process

"Why is it so hard to find a place to eat or try new items, even with apps like Yelp and Google?"

This question drove me to explore the consumer dining experience after observing similar struggles among friends and family. As a food enthusiast who values culinary diversity, I was motivated to create a solution.

Using the 5-stage Design Thinking model from Stanford's d.school, I created a prototype of TasteBuds. My process followed the 5-stage Design Thinking model from Stanford's d.school to address this issue.

5 methodology

First, I measured individual user satisfaction levels on the following: average time spent on finding a place to eat, frequency of trying new restaurants, frequency of trying new dishes, and current methods to make dining decisions. Then I assigned a scoring system, where I assigned points to each satisfaction level, with "Completely satisfied" being 5 points and both "Completely dissatisfied" & "Mostly dissatisfied" being 0 points. Due to having only one data point for "Completely dissatisfied," I combined its score with "Mostly dissatisfied" to simplify calculations and keep the ratings as whole numbers without decimals for a clearer analysis.

Competitor Analysis
Learning from my competitors

My research found that diners mostly use Yelp, Google, Google Maps, and word-of-mouth recommendations to explore options. Therefore, I analyzed Google Maps and Yelp's designs and looked into their relationship with diners' pain points.

Yelp: Food, Delivery & Reviews

Strengths

  • User-generated reviews inform decisions

Weaknesses

  • Cluttered UI, heavy sponsored content
  • Complex navigation and subpar above-the-fold
  • Unclear CTAs
  • Extensive scrolling required for key information
  • Open-ended reviews may compromise quality

Google Maps

Strengths

  • Clean and intuitive interface
  • Simple search flow and navigation
  • Clear CTAs & well executed above-the-fold design
  • Structured review process

Weaknesses

  • Lack of filtering or sorting options
  • Potential for decision paralysis, especially for location-focused diners

Conclusion

I found a significant shift in my competitors' products—from being user-centric to becoming more system-centric. Their lack of personalization and an excess of commercialization, has resulted in diners experiencing mistrust, information overload, and decision paralysis.

Meet the diners:

Given the diversity of diners' needs and behaviors, I created 3 user personas to better understand my target audience and guide my ideation phase: Alex, Brandon, and Cleo.

Alex

I enjoy dining out because I can spend quality time with my friends and family, eat delicious food, and discover new restaurants. Despite their differences, Alex, Brandon, and Cleo have something in common: They feel overwhelmed by endless search results that are irrelevant to their needs and desire more personalization in their search methods.

USER STORIES
What functionalities do diners desire?

I began brainstorming solutions by creating user stories to explore specific functionalities that could enhance the dining out experience. Below are a few key diner stories:

I want to easily get recommendations from friends, so I can confidently try new places.

I want personalized dining recommendations, so I can discover and explore options that suit me.

I want to search for options with more customization like filters, so I can avoid decision paralysis.

I want to write reviews that are visible to my friends only, so I can share my opinions comfortably.

user flow
How do I make diner stories a reality?

Given diners' habits with using mobile apps for decision-making, I decided that my product would also be a mobile application. To bring the diner stories I created to life, I brainstormed potential features, which I conceptualized into three potential routes using user flows. This process helped me begin visualizing how diners would interact with my product to enhance their dining experience!

Diner signs up and sets up their account

  • Create account with email or social
  • Input food preferences
  • Add friends

After a standard sign-up process, diners can input their food preferences then add their friends by linking their social media accounts and contacts. This would enable personalized recommendations for diners and allow diners to see dining recommendations from friends they add.

Diner searches for dining options

  • Saved search presets
  • Filter by 'Cuisines'
  • Filter by 'Visit history'
  • Personalized recommendations
  • Compatibility score (% Match)
  • Limited search results

Diners can enter keywords and preferences, filter by visit history, and see results sorted by a “% Match” score based on AI-driven preferences and dining history. Alex and Cleo, who value discovery, can exclude previously visited spots, while Brandon can choose familiar places. Frequent searches, like Brandon’s "Late-night dinner," can be saved for quick access. The search offers a finite number of results, with a refresh option to balance choice and discovery.

Diner reviews their dining experience

  • Rate ordered items (required)
  • Zero and half star rating system
  • Strengths and weaknesses of dining experience
  • Recommendation rating
  • Written review (optional)

Users can record and share detailed dining experiences through a structured review process that emphasizes critical decision-making factors. To counter reluctance in writing reviews, the flow leverages 'completion bias' to encourage genuine, food-focused feedback. Only two sections are mandatory, with the rest optional, including the written portion. To balance personalized suggestions with privacy, diners can set reviews as private to enhance recommendations without publicly sharing.

Feedback

Methodology

I recruited 5 diners to test out my high-fidelity prototype remotely and gather feedback. I sought out participants between the ages 21 and 30 who dine out at least once a week and use at least one online method to find dining options. Each test was moderated and included the following tasks:

  1. Route 1: Create an account using your Facebook login.
  2. Route 2: Find a restaurant to dine at within 3 miles of your current location.
  3. Route 3: Write a 4.5-star review with a photo for Olives Branch Bistro, where you had a Mixed Kebab Plate.

Findings

Overall, participants found the prototype very informative and intuitive! The most critical error occured during the onboarding flow, as most participants mistakened TasteBuds as a food ordering app. Nonetheless, they all highly prefer it over their current tools. I was pleased to confirm that my primary and secondary insights continue to be consistent. The visual aids were frequently affirmed in its impact on scanability, which they expressed significantly helped them complete the onboarding process.

Here's a summary of their feedback:

Design validation

  • Users praised the visual and interaction design.
  • They prefer TasteBuds over existing tools like Yelp.
  • The personalization and customization features very helpful.
  • 3 diners used the alternative routes to explore dining options.
  • They found TasteBuds informative, diverse, and inclusive.
  • Users praised the visual aids and expressed they enhanced scanability

Major errors

  • 4 diners were confused by the onboarding copy.
  • Onboarding failed to communicate the core features.
  • 3 diners struggled with the star rating system.
  • Navigation to the review page was unclear.
  • 3 diners found the review flow lengthy.

Final round of testing

Methodology

For my final round of testing, I recruited 5 new participants that shared the same characteristics as the previous round and instructed the same set of tasks. My primary objective was to see if my iterations resolved the issues that occured during the previous test. I was also interested in seeing if there are any unintended use cases.

Affinity Mapping

For this round of testing, I wanted to challenge myself and generate some quantitative data for my data synthesis. While 5 participants doesn't yield substantial data, I wanted to experiment with another tool and see if Notion could help me better identify patterns and themes via keywords. By the end of my data synthesis, I generated affinity maps for each route and organized the data using Notion's variety of view options.

Here is an overview of my process:

With Notion's features, I was able to filter and arrange data to efficiently identify patterns. For example, using the count property, I could tally participants and sort data to visualize the most recurrent issues. The single-select and multi-select tags helped me pinpoint themes and locate specific data quickly, making the process much easier.

I usually use Google Maps, so I'd type in “Mexican restaurant”. I don't think there’s really any filtering. Sometimes you can be overloaded with all the results. No other apps have filters as extensive… If I had this app, I'd totally use it all the time. I really like the different filters.

Limitations

I know most if not all case studies don’t discuss their limitations, but as a designer with a degree in sociology, it feels wrong not to include them:

  • Observer Bias: The presence of an observer during usability testing can influence participants' behavior or responses.
  • Limited Scope of Testing: While I was only testing my red routes, . This became apparent when some participants deviated from the route. Therefore, I likely missed out on specific usability issues that could occur in other situations.
  • Difficulty Capturing Emotions and Attitudes: My usability testing primarily focuses on observable behaviors and task completion, making it challenging to capture users' emotions or attitudes. Although I tried my best to extract insights through ample follow-up questions, as well as reminding users to think out loud, I was limited by time constraints.
  • Designer Bias and Subjectivity: As expressed earlier, my own biases as a user and an enthusiastic designer limited my perspective, which likely compromised the effectiveness of my tasks and limited my observations. Additionally, I inevitably interpreted test results subjectively, which led to overlooking behaviors due to preconceived biases, desire for validation, and anticipations.
  • Impact of Wording in Instructions: Subtle changes in task wording led users to interpret them differently, requiring adjustments that might have compromised data consistency.

What are limitations? Limitations in research are the boundaries or weaknesses that restrict its scope, reliability, or generalizability. They’re constraints that can hinder the study's ability to fully address certain aspects or make broad conclusions. Recognizing these limitations is important to understand the study's context and interpret its findings accurately. In other words, these are the reasons you should take my conclusions with at least an iota of salt!

Final prototype

What's next?

If I had more time, I would optimize the prototype by eliminating non-essential features, ensuring design consistency across all user flows, and enhancing the UI for the review process. I would implement and test a thumbs up/down rating system and a summary of aggregate votes per restaurant quality in profiles. Additionally, I would identify and design for edge cases, such as empty states, non-restaurant partner profiles, and users who are the first to review a non-restaurant partner in their social circle. I’m also interested in retesting the review route to evaluate how diners review a recent dining experience in a real-world setting.

If I could ship this with a team, I would prioritize refining the business strategy and develop a prototype for restaurant partners. This prototype would integrate restaurant partners into the user journey and optimize existing touchpoints based on insights from further research. We would conduct user interviews and surveys with restaurant owners, then refine and develop features that ensure seamless and intuitive interaction across the platform. The final round of testing indicates that features like gamification may not be essential for user engagement, but we would validate this hypothesis through further testing or post-beta launch analysis, considering its potential scalability benefits.

Learnings

This usability test taught me a lot about my design solutions and design thinking. I uncovered some of my most significant mistakes and weaknesses as a designer and as an individual that contributed to the issues:

Avoid overdesigning

I was trying to please everyone—rookie mistake. This led to scope creep and caused information overload for users.

Mental models can change

I was unnecessarily concerned about existing behaviors and mental models. I learned that people are willing to challenge and shift their mental models for compelling alternatives, especially for painkiller products (such as TasteBuds). After all, industry standards are always evolving. As a designer who values innovation, this was a critical and motivating lesson.

Iterate user flows

I learned the importance of iterative user flows. While many designers create only one draft at the beginning, it's important to continue iterating, which also helps track iterations.

Reflection

As my first exposure to product and UX design, my inexperience led to several challenges throughout the project. The steep learning curve with Figma resulted in poor version control, and working independently without accountability and diverse perspectives led to significant scope creep. In hindsight, my major roadblocks stemmed from overdoing anticipatory design and UI design, further contributing to scope creep.

On the bright side, this case study highlighted the value of my background in sociological research and business development. My approach to user research and testing helped me navigate iterations and manage scope creep. While my business acumen aided in designing features that support my monetization strategy, it also contributed to scope creep. Fortunately, many of these additional features have the potential to be scalable in the future.

Moving forward, I plan to strengthen my skills in designing MVPs, improving version control, and balancing business objectives without causing scope creep. I also aim to rely more on user testing than anticipatory design to maximize time and resources. Overall, this was an incredibly rewarding experience. I learned to embrace criticism and unintended use cases as opportunities for innovation. Overall, this design journey established my core values as a designer: humility, innovation, and a constant drive for growth. I look forward to applying my new skills and knowledge to future projects!
